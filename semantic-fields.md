NVC: More Than Communication  
Nonviolent Communication (NVC) is not just a method of dialogue; it's a holistic approach to  
understanding human interaction and fostering self-awareness. At its core, NVC is structured  
around four key components:  
Core Principles of Nonviolent Communication  
**Observations**: This involves describing what you observe in a situation without  
judgment or evaluation. It's about stating facts as they are, devoid of criticism or  
interpretation. For instance, instead of saying,

Language of Compassion  
NVC fosters a language of compassion and empathy. It shifts the focus from habitual  
patterns of defense and attack to understanding and acknowledging shared human needs.  
This approach promotes deeper connection and understanding in interactions by:  
● **Reducing Defensiveness**: By focusing on observations and feelings, NVC  
minimizes the likelihood of defensive reactions, opening up space for more genuine  
dialogue.  
● **Building Empathy**: NVC encourages individuals to not only express their own  
feelings and needs but also to empathetically recognize and respond to the feelings  
and needs of others.  
● **Creating Constructive Outcomes**: Through its emphasis on specific requests, NVC  
guides conversations towards finding constructive and mutually satisfying solutions.  
By integrating these principles into communication, NVC transforms interactions from  
potential conflicts into opportunities for understanding, connection, and collaboration. It is a  
powerful tool for navigating the complexities of human relationships, fostering a deeper  
sense of connection and community.  
NVC as a Semantic Field Technique  
Nonviolent Communication (NVC) is more than a technique for effective dialogue; it's a  
pathway to deeper self-discovery. By encouraging individuals to articulate their feelings and  
needs clearly, NVC offers a framework for personal insight and growth.  
Articulating Feelings and Needs  
● **Clarity of Self-Expression**: NVC compels individuals to explore and express their  
feelings and needs accurately. This process often leads to revelations about personal  
motivations, desires, and values that may have been previously unacknowledged or  
misunderstood.  
● **Emotional Intelligence**: Through the practice of NVC, individuals develop greater  
emotional intelligence. They become more adept at identifying and naming their  
emotions, understanding the link between their feelings and their deeper needs.  
● **Self-Awareness and Growth**: As individuals use NVC to express themselves, they  
gain insights into their behavioral patterns and emotional triggers. This awareness is  
the first step towards personal development and change.Transforming Conversations with NVC  
NVC has the power to transform the nature of conversations, shifting them from  
confrontational or disconnected exchanges to interactions that are collaborative and  
empathetic.  
From Conflict to Collaboration  
NVC lies in its ability to shift the dynamics of interactions from conflict-oriented to  
collaboration-focused. This transition is rooted in the way NVC reframes conversations,  
emphasizing feelings and needs over accusations and judgments.  
Reframing Interactions  
Detecting and Changing Abusive Vocabulary: NVC helps in identifying language patterns  
that are accusatory or judgmental \- what we might term 'abusive vocabulary'  
. By recognizing  
these patterns, individuals can consciously shift to a vocabulary that focuses on expressing  
feelings and needs, thus reframing the interaction.  
● **Semantic Field Shift**: This change in vocabulary represents a shift in the semantic  
field from one that is conflict-laden to one that fosters understanding. It’s about  
moving from a language of blame to a language of personal experience and shared  
humanity.  
● **De-escalating Conflicts**: By focusing on what is being felt and needed, rather than  
on who is right or wrong, NVC de-escalates potential conflicts. It opens up space for  
empathy and mutual understanding, which are the cornerstones of collaborative  
interactions.  
Constructive Problem Solving  
● **Expressing Needs and Requests**: NVC teaches individuals to articulate their needs  
and make clear requests. This approach is solution-oriented, moving away from the  
battleground of arguments to a collaborative space where everyone’s needs are  
considered.  
● **Shifting Focus in Conversations**: Instead of conversations being a platform for  
asserting dominance or winning points, NVC transforms them into opportunities for  
collaborative problem-solving. It’s about finding mutually satisfying solutions rather  
than one-sided victories.  
● **Building Consensus**: By centering conversations around needs and requests, NVC  
fosters an environment where consensus can be reached more easily. It encourages  
a give-and-take approach, where all parties are seen and heard.  
Empathy and Connection  
● **Fostering Empathy**: NVC encourages active listening and empathy. By trying to  
understand and acknowledge the feelings and needs of others, a deeper level of  
empathy is cultivated in conversations.● **Building Stronger Relationships**: This empathetic approach fosters stronger, more  
meaningful relationships. Conversations are no longer transactional but are  
transformed into opportunities for genuine connection and understanding.

The Role of Active Participation in Memory WiringIntroduction to Neuroscience Vocabulary  
Before diving into how active participation affects memory wiring, let's introduce some key  
technical terms from neuroscience that will be pivotal in our understanding:  
● **Neuroplasticity**: The brain's ability to reorganize itself by forming new neural  
connections throughout life. Neuroplasticity allows the neurons (nerve cells) in the  
brain to compensate for injury and disease and to adjust their activities in response to  
new situations or changes in their environment.  
● **Synapse**: A synapse is a structure that permits a neuron (or nerve cell) to pass an  
electrical or chemical signal to another neuron.  
● **Dendrite**: These are the branched extensions of a neuron that act to conduct the  
electrochemical stimulation received from other neural cells to the cell body, or soma,  
of the neuron from which the dendrites project.

The introduction of a structured micro-schedule in therapy sessions is a strategic approach

engagement with the neo-vocabulary, facilitates comprehensive progress tracking, and

For Adolescents/Game Players  
● 2-minute recap of the previous session (led by therapist notes): Begin with a quick  
overview of the last session, focusing on game-related achievements or challenges  
discussed.  
● 2-minute player's log (participant’s recap of week achievements and challenges):  
Encourage the adolescent to share their weekly experiences using game  
terminology, highlighting any "levels" they've conquered or obstacles they've faced.  
● 1-minute vocabulary quest recap (neo-vocabulary review): Review any new terms  
introduced in the previous session, relating them back to game mechanics or stories  
to reinforce their meaning.  
● 40-minute quest journey (free speech/conversation): Dive into the main conversation,  
allowing the adolescent to guide the session through their narrative, using the  
session to explore deeper themes through the metaphor of a game.  
● 5-minute level up discussion (process improvement): End the session by discussing  
what worked well, what could be improved, and any new words or concepts to  
introduce in the next "level" of therapy.  
For Elderly/Aging Adults  
● 2-minute reflection on past discussions (started by therapist notes): Briefly revisit the  
key points from the last session, especially any life stories or wisdom shared.  
● 2-minute week in review (participant’s recap of achievements and challenges): Give  
the patient time to reflect on their week, encouraging them to share any significant  
memories or feelings that have arisen.  
● 1-minute vocabulary reflection (neo-vocabulary recap): Go over any new vocabulary  
introduced, linking it to their life experiences or reflections for better integration.  
● 40-minute storytelling time (free speech/conversation): Allocate the bulk of the  
session for the patient to share stories, experiences, or thoughts, with the therapist  
guiding the discussion to explore deeper emotional insights.● 5-minute legacy building (process improvement): Conclude with feedback on the  
session's approach and discuss any adjustments or new themes to explore in future  
sessions.  
For Career-Focused Individuals  
● 2-minute professional recap (initiated by therapist notes): Start with a brief overview  
of the career-related topics discussed in the previous session.  
● 2-minute career checkpoint (participant’s recap of professional achievements and  
challenges): Allow the individual to share their professional progress or any  
challenges they've faced, linking back to previously set goals.  
● 1-minute linguistic strategy review (neo-vocabulary recap): Quickly review any new

● 40-minute strategic dialogue (free speech/conversation): Use this time for an open

● 5-minute action plan adjustment (process improvement): End with a brief feedback

therapeutic tools. Rejecting the prevalent methods of electroshock and lobotomy, da Silveira

art to express complex, often ineffable emotional and psychological states, bridging the gap  
between the conscious and the unconscious.

of her work. Jung's analytical psychology, with its emphasis on archetypes and the collective  
unconscious, found a resonant echo in da Silveira's art therapy practice. Together, they  
explored the deep symbology manifest in the artworks of her patients, offering  
groundbreaking insights into the human psyche.
